# Atrial Fibrillation

## Overview

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia, affecting approximately 2-4% of the adult population. Prevalence increases significantly with age, reaching 5-8% in individuals over 65 years.

**Source:** European Heart Journal, 2023 ESC Guidelines for AF Management.

## Epidemiology & Risk Factors

- **Age:** Primary risk factor; prevalence doubles with each decade after 55.
- **Hypertension:** Present in ~60% of AF patients.
- **Heart failure:** AF and HF frequently coexist (30-40%).
- **Obesity:** BMI >30 increases AF risk by 49%.
- **Diabetes mellitus:** 20-25% of AF patients have DM.

**Source:** Framingham Heart Study longitudinal data, 2022.

## Stroke Risk Assessment

The **CHA₂DS₂-VASc** score is the standard tool for stroke risk stratification:

| Factor | Points |
|--------|--------|
| Congestive heart failure | 1 |
| Hypertension | 1 |
| Age ≥75 | 2 |
| Diabetes mellitus | 1 |
| Stroke/TIA history | 2 |
| Vascular disease | 1 |
| Age 65-74 | 1 |
| Sex (female) | 1 |

**Clinical Decision:** Score ≥2 (male) or ≥3 (female) → oral anticoagulation recommended.

**Source:** CHA₂DS₂-VASc validation study, Lip et al., 2010.

## Anticoagulation Strategy

- **NOACs** (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, dabigatran) are preferred over warfarin.
- **Warfarin** remains indicated for mechanical valve prostheses.
- **Aspirin alone** is no longer recommended for stroke prevention in AF.

**Source:** NOAC meta-analysis, Ruff et al., Lancet 2022.

## Clinical Implications

1. All AF patients should undergo CHA₂DS₂-VASc scoring at first diagnosis.
2. Rate vs rhythm control strategy should be individualized.
3. Catheter ablation is a Class I recommendation for drug-refractory symptomatic AF.

## Limitations

- CHA₂DS₂-VASc does not account for renal function.
- NOAC dosing requires eGFR monitoring (especially dabigatran).
- Long-term outcomes of early rhythm control (EAST-AFNET 4) need further validation.
